Modern Indian History

From Colonial Rule to the Republic — 1757 to 1960s

Colonial Rule Established
1600 — 1856
1600 East India Company Founded Economic
SSB Key Event

The English East India Company was granted a Royal Charter by Queen Elizabeth I to trade in the East. It initially competed with Portuguese, Dutch and French traders for control of Indian spice, cotton and silk trade. The first English factory was set up on the banks of the river Hugli in 1651, which eventually grew into Calcutta (Kolkata).

Key Points for SSB
  • Company was a mercantile trading body, not initially a ruling power
  • First factory at Hugli, 1651 → later grew into Calcutta
  • Competed with Portuguese, Dutch, and French East India Companies
  • Obtained zamindari rights over 3 villages including Kalikata from Mughals
1757 Battle of Plassey Military
SSB Key Event

Robert Clive led the Company's forces against Nawab Sirajuddaulah of Bengal at Plassey. The Nawab was betrayed by his own commander Mir Jafar, who never fought the battle in return for being made the next Nawab. This was the first major Company military victory in India and the beginning of British political dominance over Bengal.

Key Points for SSB
  • Robert Clive defeated Sirajuddaulah, Nawab of Bengal
  • Mir Jafar betrayed the Nawab and was made the new puppet Nawab
  • First major Company military victory in India
  • Marked shift from trade to political power
  • Plassey = anglicised "Palashi", named after the palash tree
1764 Battle of Buxar Military

Mir Qasim (who replaced Mir Jafar) rebelled against Company interference and allied with the Nawab of Awadh and the Mughal Emperor. He was defeated at Buxar. The victory was more decisive than Plassey and proved the Company's military superiority over Indian rulers.

Key Points for SSB
  • Fought between Company and combined forces of Mir Qasim, Nawab of Awadh, and Mughal Emperor Shah Alam II
  • Company victory → more decisive than Plassey
  • Led directly to Diwani grant in 1765
1765 Company becomes Diwan of Bengal Political
SSB Key Event

On 12 August 1765, the Mughal Emperor appointed the East India Company as the Diwan of Bengal, Bihar and Orissa. As Diwan, the Company became the chief financial administrator — it could now collect and use Bengal's vast revenues. This was a turning point: from trading company to territorial colonial power. Robert Clive declared "We must indeed become nawabs ourselves."

Key Points for SSB
  • Diwani = right to collect land revenue of Bengal, Bihar, Orissa
  • Company no longer needed to import gold from Britain to buy Indian goods
  • Revenues from India now financed Company's army, trade and expansion
  • Transition from trade to territorial rule begins here
1773 Warren Hastings — First Governor-General Political

The Regulating Act of 1773 established the post of Governor-General of India. Warren Hastings became the first Governor-General. This marked the beginning of direct British government oversight of the Company's rule in India, replacing purely commercial management.

Key Points for SSB
  • First Governor-General of India: Warren Hastings (1773)
  • Regulating Act, 1773 — British Parliament's first regulation of Company
  • Began formalisation of colonial administration
1857 Doctrine of Lapse — Dalhousie's Expansion Political

Lord Dalhousie implemented the Doctrine of Lapse — if an Indian ruler died without a natural heir, the kingdom would be "lapsed" (annexed) to the Company. States like Satara, Nagpur, Jhansi and others were annexed. This policy deeply angered Indian rulers including the Rani of Jhansi, fuelling the fires of the 1857 revolt.

Key Points for SSB
  • Dalhousie: Governor-General 1848–1856
  • Doctrine of Lapse: annexed states without natural heirs
  • Annexed: Satara (1848), Jhansi (1854), Nagpur (1854), Awadh (1856)
  • Major cause of the Revolt of 1857
The Revolt of 1857
1857 — 1858
1857 Outbreak of the Great Revolt Military
SSB Key Event

The immediate trigger was the introduction of the Enfield rifle cartridges greased with animal fat — offensive to both Hindu and Muslim soldiers. The revolt began at Meerut on May 10, 1857, when sepoys refused to use the cartridges, were imprisoned, and then freed by their fellow soldiers. The rebellion spread rapidly across north and central India.

Key Points for SSB
  • Started at Meerut, 10 May 1857 by Indian sepoys
  • Immediate cause: greased Enfield cartridges (cow/pig fat)
  • Deeper causes: Doctrine of Lapse, land revenue policies, cultural interference
  • Key leaders: Rani Lakshmi Bai (Jhansi), Nana Sahib (Kanpur), Bahadur Shah Zafar (Delhi), Tantia Tope, Begum Hazrat Mahal (Lucknow)
  • Known as: First War of Independence / Sepoy Mutiny / Revolt of 1857
1858 British Crown Takes Direct Control Political
SSB Key Event

After suppressing the revolt by end of 1859, the British Parliament passed a new Act in 1858 transferring all powers from the East India Company to the British Crown. The Governor-General was given the title of Viceroy. Queen Victoria's Proclamation promised to respect the rights of all Indian rulers and non-interference in religious matters.

Key Points for SSB
  • Government of India Act, 1858 — Company rule ended
  • British Crown / Queen Victoria assumed direct rule over India
  • Governor-General now called Viceroy (representative of the Crown)
  • Secretary of State for India appointed, advised by India Council
  • Proportion of Indian soldiers reduced; more Gurkhas, Sikhs, Pathans recruited
  • Ruling chiefs assured: no more annexations; could pass kingdoms to heirs
Rise of the National Movement
1870s — 1915
1885 Indian National Congress Founded National
SSB Key Event

The Indian National Congress (INC) was founded in December 1885 in Bombay, with A.O. Hume, a retired British civil servant, playing a key role. It began as an organisation of educated Indians — lawyers, journalists and professionals — who sought greater rights and representation through petitions and constitutional methods. Early leaders (Moderates) included Dadabhai Naoroji, Bal Gangadhar Tilak, Gopal Krishna Gokhale and Pherozeshah Mehta.

Key Points for SSB
  • Founded: December 1885, Bombay
  • Founders include A.O. Hume (retired ICS officer)
  • Moderates: sought constitutional reforms through petitions — Gokhale, Mehta, Naoroji
  • Radicals/Extremists: demanded self-rule, used boycott — Tilak, Lajpat Rai, Bipin Pal
  • Split in 1907 at Surat; reunited in December 1915
1905 Partition of Bengal & Swadeshi Movement National
SSB Key Event

Lord Curzon announced the Partition of Bengal on religious lines (Hindu West / Muslim East Bengal) on 16 October 1905. This was seen as a British attempt to divide the nationalist movement. It sparked the Swadeshi Movement — boycott of British goods and use of Indian-made (swadeshi) products. The movement introduced mass politics into the independence struggle.

Key Points for SSB
  • Viceroy Curzon partitioned Bengal: 1905 (reversed in 1911)
  • Swadeshi = use of Indian goods; boycott of British goods
  • First major mass agitation in India
  • Led by Bal Gangadhar Tilak, Bipin Chandra Pal, Lala Lajpat Rai ("Lal-Bal-Pal")
1906 Muslim League Founded Political

The All India Muslim League was established in Dhaka in 1906. It initially sought to protect Muslim political interests under British rule. The Lucknow Pact (1916) saw the Congress and Muslim League cooperate for representative government. However, by the 1930s–40s the League, under Jinnah, demanded a separate Muslim nation.

Key Points for SSB
  • Founded: 1906, Dhaka
  • Lucknow Pact, 1916: Congress–League alliance for self-rule
  • Pakistan Resolution, 1940 (Lahore): demanded separate Muslim states
  • Muhammad Ali Jinnah: key leader of the League
1915 Gandhi Returns to India from South Africa National
SSB Key Event

Mahatma Gandhi returned to India in 1915 at age 46, already famous for leading Indians in South Africa against racial discrimination using non-violent resistance (Satyagraha). He spent his first year travelling India, understanding its people. His first major interventions were in Champaran (Bihar), Kheda (Gujarat) and Ahmedabad — establishing his method of peaceful mass resistance.

Key Points for SSB
  • Gandhi returned from South Africa, 1915
  • Satyagraha = truth-force / non-violent resistance
  • Champaran Satyagraha (1917): against indigo planters — first civil disobedience in India
  • Kheda Satyagraha (1918): peasant agitation against tax during crop failure
  • Ahmedabad mill workers' strike, 1918
The Gandhi Era & Mass Movements
1919 — 1947
1919 Jallianwala Bagh Massacre Military
SSB Key Event

Against the Rowlatt Act (1919) which curbed civil liberties, Gandhi called the first all-India Satyagraha on 6 April 1919. General Dyer opened fire on a peaceful, unarmed assembly in the Jallianwala Bagh, Amritsar on Baisakhi Day (13 April 1919), killing hundreds. Rabindranath Tagore renounced his knighthood in protest. This massacre radicalised Indian public opinion against British rule.

Key Points for SSB
  • Rowlatt Act, 1919: curtailed fundamental rights; called "Black Act"
  • Jallianwala Bagh: 13 April 1919, Amritsar; General Dyer ordered firing
  • Rabindranath Tagore renounced his knighthood in protest
  • Rowlatt Satyagraha: first all-India agitation against British
1920–22 Non-Cooperation Movement National
SSB Key Event

Gandhi launched the Non-Cooperation Movement combining protest against the Jallianwala Bagh massacre and the Khilafat issue (British treatment of the Ottoman Caliph). Thousands of students left British schools, lawyers gave up practice, people boycotted foreign cloth. Gandhi called off the movement after the Chauri Chaura incident (February 1922) in which a crowd burnt a police station killing 22 policemen.

Key Points for SSB
  • Launched 1920; combined Khilafat and anti-British grievances
  • Forms: boycott of schools, courts, legislatures, foreign cloth
  • Called off after Chauri Chaura incident, February 1922
  • Khilafat leaders: Mohammad Ali and Shaukat Ali
  • First truly mass-scale national movement
1929 Purna Swaraj Resolution — Lahore Congress National
SSB Key Event

Under the presidentship of Jawaharlal Nehru, the Indian National Congress passed the Purna Swaraj (Complete Independence) resolution at the Lahore session in December 1929. 26 January 1930 was declared as "Independence Day" and observed all over India — a date that would later become Republic Day.

Key Points for SSB
  • Lahore Session, December 1929: Purna Swaraj declared
  • Presided over by Jawaharlal Nehru
  • 26 January 1930 observed as Independence Day (later became Republic Day)
  • Prior to this, Congress had only demanded Dominion Status (Home Rule)
1930 Dandi March — Salt Satyagraha National
SSB Key Event

Gandhi led a 240-mile march from Sabarmati Ashram to the coastal town of Dandi to break the salt law — which gave the British state a monopoly on salt production and taxed it heavily. On 6 April 1930, Gandhi gathered natural salt at the seashore, launching the Civil Disobedience Movement. The march united rich and poor, as salt was a universal necessity. Women participated in large numbers.

Key Points for SSB
  • Dandi March: 12 March – 6 April 1930
  • 240 miles from Sabarmati (Ahmedabad) to Dandi (Gujarat coast)
  • Broke salt law: challenged British taxation on a basic necessity
  • Civil Disobedience Movement launched; mass arrests followed
  • Gandhi–Irwin Pact (1931) followed; Gandhi attended 2nd Round Table Conference in London
1928–31 Bhagat Singh & Revolutionary Nationalism National
SSB Key Event

Bhagat Singh, Chandra Shekhar Azad, Sukhdev and Rajguru founded the Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (HSRA) in 1928. In December 1928, Bhagat Singh and Rajguru assassinated British police officer Saunders who was involved in the lathi-charge that caused the death of Lala Lajpat Rai. On 8 April 1929, Bhagat Singh and B.K. Dutt threw a bomb in the Central Legislative Assembly (not to kill, but "to make the deaf hear"). Bhagat Singh, Sukhdev and Rajguru were hanged on 23 March 1931. Bhagat Singh was only 23 years old.

Key Points for SSB
  • HSRA founded 1928 at Ferozeshah Kotla, Delhi
  • Lala Lajpat Rai died after lathi-charge during Simon Commission protests, 1928
  • Bhagat Singh and Rajguru: assassinated Saunders (December 1928)
  • Bomb in Central Legislative Assembly: 8 April 1929
  • Executed: 23 March 1931; Bhagat Singh aged 23
1942 Quit India Movement National
SSB Key Event

During World War II, Gandhi launched the Quit India Movement on 8 August 1942, demanding immediate British withdrawal. His call: "Do or Die." Gandhi and all Congress leaders were immediately arrested. The movement spread across India — peasants, youth, workers attacked symbols of British authority. By end 1943, over 90,000 people were arrested and around 1,000 killed in police firing. The rebellion brought the Raj to its knees.

Key Points for SSB
  • Launched: 8 August 1942, Bombay session of Congress
  • Gandhi's slogan: "Do or Die" (Karo ya Maro)
  • All Congress leaders arrested immediately
  • 90,000+ arrested; ~1,000 killed in police firing
  • Subhas Chandra Bose formed Indian National Army (INA/Azad Hind Fauj) in 1941–43
Independence & the Republic
1947 — 1960s
15 Aug 1947 India's Independence & Partition National
SSB Key Event

India achieved independence at midnight on 14–15 August 1947. But independence came with the partition of the subcontinent into India and Pakistan. The partition caused one of history's largest forced migrations: 8 million refugees entered India. Hundreds of thousands were killed in communal riots. India's population was ~345 million across nearly 500 princely states that had to be integrated.

Key Points for SSB
  • Independence Day: 15 August 1947
  • Mountbatten Plan / 3 June Plan: announced partition of India and Pakistan
  • 8 million refugees entered India from Pakistan
  • Jawaharlal Nehru: first Prime Minister of India
  • Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel: integrated ~500 princely states into India
  • Muhammad Ali Jinnah: first Governor-General of Pakistan
30 Jan 1948 Assassination of Mahatma Gandhi National

Mahatma Gandhi — the Father of the Nation — was assassinated by Nathuram Godse on 30 January 1948, less than six months after independence. Godse disagreed with Gandhi's conviction that Hindus and Muslims should live together in harmony. Nehru announced to the nation: "The light has gone out of our lives and there is darkness everywhere."

Key Points for SSB
  • Assassinated: 30 January 1948 at Birla House, New Delhi
  • Assassin: Nathuram Godse
  • Title: Father of the Nation (Rashtra Pita)
  • Nehru's words: "The light has gone out of our lives…" — broadcast on AIR
26 Jan 1950 Constitution of India Comes into Force Political
SSB Key Event

After nearly three years of deliberations by the Constituent Assembly (December 1946 – November 1949), the Indian Constitution came into force on 26 January 1950 — chosen to honour the 1930 Independence Day declaration. India became a Sovereign Democratic Republic. Dr. B.R. Ambedkar was the chief architect of the Constitution. Universal adult franchise was granted to all Indians above 21.

Key Points for SSB
  • Constitution adopted: 26 November 1949; enforced: 26 January 1950
  • Dr. B.R. Ambedkar: Chairman of Drafting Committee — "Father of the Constitution"
  • Universal Adult Franchise: all Indians above 21 could vote (lowered to 18 in 1989)
  • Abolished untouchability, guaranteed equality before law
  • Special provisions for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes
  • India declared a Sovereign Socialist Secular Democratic Republic
1950–56 Planning Commission & Five Year Plans Economic

In 1950, the Government of India set up the Planning Commission under PM Nehru to design economic development policy. India adopted a "mixed economy" model (both state and private sector). The First Five Year Plan (1951–56) focused on agriculture. The Second Five Year Plan (1956–61) prioritised heavy industry — steel plants at Bhilai, Durgapur, and Rourkela were established.

Key Points for SSB
  • Planning Commission set up: 1950, under PM Nehru
  • Mixed economy: both State and private sector involvement
  • First Five Year Plan (1951–56): focus on agriculture
  • Second Five Year Plan (1956–61): heavy industry — Bhilai, Durgapur, Rourkela steel plants
  • Planning Commission replaced by NITI Aayog in 2015
1953–56 Linguistic Reorganisation of States Political

Potti Sriramulu died on a hunger fast demanding a separate Andhra state for Telugu speakers (15 December 1952). Widespread protests forced the government to create Andhra State on 1 October 1953 — the first linguistically organised state. The States Reorganisation Commission (1955) recommended redrawing state boundaries on linguistic lines. The States Reorganisation Act (1956) reorganised India's states.

Key Points for SSB
  • Potti Sriramulu: died 15 December 1952 on fast for Telugu state
  • Andhra State formed: 1 October 1953 — first linguistic state
  • States Reorganisation Act, 1956 — reorganised all states on linguistic basis
  • Punjab divided into Punjab and Haryana in 1966
1955 Bandung Conference — Non-Alignment Political

India under Nehru adopted a foreign policy of Non-Alignment — refusing to join either the US-led NATO or the Soviet-led Warsaw Pact. At the Bandung Conference (1955), 29 Asian and African nations met in Indonesia under Nehru, Nasser (Egypt), Sukarno (Indonesia), Nkrumah (Ghana) and others to oppose colonialism and stay out of Cold War blocs. This became the Non-Aligned Movement (NAM).

Key Points for SSB
  • Non-Alignment: India's foreign policy — neither US nor Soviet bloc
  • Bandung Conference: April 1955, Indonesia — 29 Afro-Asian nations
  • Key architects: Nehru (India), Nasser (Egypt), Tito (Yugoslavia)
  • Panchsheel (Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence) — Nehru & Zhou Enlai, 1954
  • Krishna Menon led India's delegation to UN 1952–62
