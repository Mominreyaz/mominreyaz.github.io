1.1

Introduction

Cost Accounting is a specialised branch of accounting. It is concerned with classifying, recording and appropriate allocation of expenditure for the determination of costs of products and services and the ascertainment of profitability. It also embraces the preparation of periodical statements and reports for purposes of controlling costs and managerial decision-making.

In this unit, we shall introduce you to the nature and scope of cost accounting and discuss in detail: the need for cost accounting, its definition and objects, the difference between cost accounting and financial accounting, the advantages of cost accounting, and the installation of a costing system in an organisation.

1.2

Need for Costing

Every economic activity, particularly an activity involved in the production of goods or in rendering service, involves some expenditure. The major purpose of such activities in a business enterprise is to earn profit. It is, therefore, necessary that all three elements of the transaction — cost, profit and price — are clearly identified.

Example A shoe factory: Material ₹20 + Labour ₹30 + Overheads ₹25 = Cost ₹75. Selling price ₹100. Profit = ₹25 per pair.

Various deficiencies and limitations of financial accounting gave rise to the need for cost accounting.

1.2.1 Limitations of Financial Accounting

  1. Does not provide detailed operating information for each department, process, or product. It only provides information for the enterprise as a whole.
  2. Does not classify expenses into direct and indirect or fixed and variable.
  3. Does not set up a proper system of control over materials and labour — wastages go unchecked.
  4. Does not establish standards or norms against which different cost items can be compared.
  5. Does not provide adequate costing information for fixation of selling price.
  6. Contains only historical cost information compiled at year-end — frequent cost data unavailable.
  7. Since product-wise cost and profit information is not available, analysis of causes for profit or loss cannot be effectively done.

1.2.2 Costing and the Economy

  1. Global Competition: Only producers who exercise stringent cost control and follow sound pricing policies can survive.
  2. Limited Resources: Acute scarcity of resources requires effective and economic utilisation by curtailing wastages.
  3. Complex Management: Industrial management demands attention and action at every stage of operation.
  4. Fast Decisions: Correct and quick decisions require adequate information supported by reliable data.
  5. Special Responsibility: Every business has social responsibility — proper quality, reasonable prices, regular supply.
  6. Optimum Profit: Business ventures aim at maximisation of profit through efficient financial, personnel, production and marketing activities.
1.3

Definitions of Costing and Cost Accounting

Costing (Wheldon) The classifying, recording and appropriate allocation of expenditure for the determination of the costs of products or services; and for the presentation suitably of arranged data for purposes of control and guidance of management.
Cost Accounting (ICMA, UK) That part of management accounting which establishes budgets and standard costs and actual costs of operations, processes, departments or products and the analysis of variances, profitability or social use of funds.

Thus, Cost Accounting:

  • Is a process of accounting for costs
  • Incorporates incomes and expenditures relating to production of goods or services
  • Provides statistical data on which future estimates may be prepared
  • Serves as a basis of ascertainment and control of costs
  • Involves the functions of analysis, recording, establishment of budgets and standards, comparison, and reporting

1.4

Objects of Cost Accounting

  1. To ascertain the cost of products and services
  2. To aid in fixation of selling price or quotation for tenders
  3. To analyse and classify the different elements of cost
  4. To identify causes of wastage and apply appropriate remedial action
  5. To control costs by analysis and comparisons
  6. To communicate cost information to management and facilitate decision-making
  7. To judge relative efficiency of different departments, branches, products, units, plants and machinery
  8. To produce cost statements as and when required for interim review or planning future activities
1.5

Difference: Cost Accounting vs Financial Accounting

# Cost Accounting Financial Accounting
1 Ascertains the cost of goods or services Presents the operational results and financial position of the business
2 Serves the information needs of management Provides financial information to shareholders, creditors, employees & society
3 Need not be followed by external audit Needs independent audit to ensure true and fair view
4 Classifies into material, labour, fixed & variable overheads Classifies all receipts and payments into debit and credit
5 Shows expenses in cost sheet, process account, etc. Consolidates into Trading & P&L Account and Balance Sheet
6 Does not form a basis for tax assessment Acts as a basis for determination of tax liabilities
7 Requires variance analysis (standard vs actual cost) Records only actual transactions
8 Presents cost information at frequent intervals Presents financial information once or twice a year
9 Finds profit/loss on specific products, branches, departments Gives operational results of entire business
10 Maximises future efficiency using cost data Uses accounting ratios to compute trends of previous period
11 Accounts for physical units such as labour hours, machine hours Follows only monetary units for recording transactions
Common Goal

Despite the differences, both systems have a common goal of continuously assisting the organisation they serve. In fact, the two are complementary to each other.

1.6

Advantages of Cost Accounting

An effective and organised system of costing helps:

  1. Continuous flow of information regarding production, cost, materials, labour, stores, plant capacity, etc., which assist output planning.
  2. Identification of unproductive activities, losses or wastage of resources, and obsolete machinery.
  3. Compilation of correct and reliable cost data.
  4. Preparation of budgets and business forecasts.
  5. Measurement of efficiency through establishment of standards and analysis of variances.
  6. Fixation of selling prices.
  7. Cost comparisons between different periods, products, departments or firms.
  8. Estimates of costs and revenue in advance.
  9. Inventory control and periodical stock-taking.
  10. Identification of idle capacity and cost of working below installed capacity.
  11. Ascertainment of cost and profit more frequently.
  12. Decisions and policies on: level of output, make or buy decisions, replacement of equipment, shutdown decisions, new product introduction, acceptance of special orders, replacement of labour with machinery.

Because of these advantages, costing is now used not just in manufacturing but also in hospitals, transport undertakings, local authorities, offices, banks, and educational institutions.

1.7

Installation of a Costing System

A single system of costing cannot suit every business. The principles and procedures of costing have to be applied in each organisation according to its own characteristics. Before introducing a system of costing, a preliminary investigation should assess the exact requirements in respect of: (a) product, (b) organisation, (c) manufacturing process, and (d) selling and distribution methods.

It should also ensure that:

  • The existing organisation is disturbed to the minimum
  • The system is implemented gradually
  • The costing process is compact and goes into meaningful details only
  • The procedure is simple and economical to operate
  • The system is able to generate periodical reports to various levels of management

1.7.1 Possible Difficulties

  1. Inadequate support from top management and opposition from some officers
  2. Resistance from staff associated with the financial accounting system
  3. Resentment at other levels due to additional work expected
  4. Shortage of trained and qualified staff to handle the new system
  5. Heavy costs involved in the process of installation

1.7.2 Factors to be Considered

  1. Objective of the costing system
  2. Nature of the business
  3. Quality of the management
  4. Size and type of organisation, scope of authority, sources of information
  5. Technical aspect of the business
  6. Attitude and behaviour of the staff
  7. Impact of different operations on variable expenses
  8. Manner of reconciliation between cost and financial accounts
  9. Quantum of information needs and data collection process
  10. Nature of the product and the type of costing system which will suit
  11. Extent to which the importance of the system can be appreciated by supporting staff

1.7.3 Success of the Costing System

The requisites of an effective system of costing are:

  1. It suits the nature and requirements of the business
  2. It is simple and easy to operate
  3. It receives full cooperation of the staff
  4. It ensures promptness and regularity in flow of required information
  5. It is closely linked with the financial accounting system
  6. It contributes to cost control effectively
  7. It provides for comparison of estimates with actual results
  8. There is considerable flexibility to adjust with changing conditions
  9. The cost of installation is justified by the benefits derived

1.8

Let Us Sum Up

Summary

Costing is a specialised branch of accounting responsible for the ascertainment and control of costs. Its need arose from limitations of financial accounting and complexities of modern enterprise management. Its main objects are: (i) ascertainment of costs, (ii) fixation of prices, (iii) control of costs, and (iv) providing cost data for managerial use. For installation, a costing system must: suit the nature of the business, be simple to understand, be introduced gradually, provide information promptly, be economical, and be flexible.

1.9

Key Words

Allocation Distribution of expenditure among various cost centres.
Budget An estimate of allocations in respect of expenditure and/or revenue during a given future period.
Costing The technique and process of ascertaining costs.
Cost Sheet A statement showing different elements of cost relating to a particular product or job for a particular period.
Cost Centre A location, person, equipment or department for which costs may be ascertained and used for control.
Direct Expenses Expenses incurred in connection with material, labour, etc. which can be directly identified with items produced.
Fixed Expenses Expenses which do not increase or decrease with a change in volume of output.
Variable Expenses Expenses which increase or decrease in the same proportion as the change in output.
Idle Capacity Unutilised production capacity of an enterprise.
Overheads All types of indirect expenses.
Variance Difference between standard cost and actual cost.
Reconciliation Matching two results by locating causes of difference and making them agree through adjustments.
Standard Pre-determined level of performance or cost based on previous experience.
Unit Cost Total cost divided by the output quantity.
Quotation Minimum acceptable price offered for the supply of goods or services.
Indirect Expenses Overheads relating to manufacturing, administration, distribution or selling which cannot be directly identified.
